Casilio steps aside — and the 50-unit project behind Samuel's clears the board 4–0.

Four years, four designs (40 → 24 → 20 → 50), and on Wednesday morning the rezoning and special exception use permit needed for 8750 Main Street Associates' multifamily housing project behind the Samuel's Grande Manor banquet hall both passed on roll-call vote — with Supervisor Patrick Casilio recused on the record because he owns the adjoining property. Three public speakers raised traffic, drainage, and a school-district privacy-fence ask. The applicant declined the fence. The board also amended a sidewalk bond up by $1.115 million, and the supervisor used eight minutes of resolution time to read a Bee-bound article punching back at municipal garbage-district advocates.

The Lead — 8750 Main Street: Rezoning, SEUP, and a Recusal

When the board reached agenda item 11, Supervisor Patrick Casilio stopped the meeting and stepped down from the dais. "I'm recusing myself from it because I own the property next door to it,"[1] he told the room. Councilman Peter DiCostanzo took the gavel. The first of two public hearings on 8750 Main Street Associates LLC's project followed immediately.

Director of Community Development Jonathan Bleuer[*] walked the project history. In 2021, property owner Charlie Pizzino proposed 40 units; the town's review brought that down to 24, and Pizzino called the smaller project cost-prohibitive and stepped away in 2023. A 20-unit revival in 2024 was withdrawn during review. The current 50-unit proposal entered planning board review in May 2025 with a different design, iterated through fall, drew a SEQR negative declaration in February 2026, picked up a Zoning Board of Appeals variance for building size, and was granted planning board concept-plan approval with 16 conditions on April 1.[1]

Sean Hopkins of Hopkins Sorgi appeared as counsel for the applicant alongside Rich and Pat McNamara. Hopkins argued the requested rezoning of the rear 1.66 acres from Residential Single-Family to Traditional Neighborhood District was "extremely well vetted" and noted his client had developed an alternative design that would not require the rezoning — if the rezoning were rejected, the project still moves forward, with a worse layout. Construction is targeted for August.[1]

Three speakers took the public-comment microphone:

Hopkins addressed all three from the podium. Traffic: detailed plan reviewed by NYSDOT engineer Kevin Hebert; written confirmation no significant adverse traffic impact. Septic versus sewer: septic, location at the rear corner, Erie County Health Department application already filed. Drainage: handled on-site to DEC and town standards including a 100-year storm event, designed to also handle water currently entering the site.[1]

On the school district's privacy-fence ask, Hopkins declined: "We'd be a little bit reluctant to clear the vegetation on the back of the site and replace that with a privacy fence. A lot of that vegetation is actually on our site and our goal would be to preserve as much of that as possible."[1] Bleuer added that a required landscape plan will go before the town's landscape review committee — representatives were in the room listening — and that the applicant would communicate with the school district directly.

The second hearing — on the SEUP itself — drew no public speakers. Both resolutions passed by roll call with Casilio recused. He returned to the dais for the rest of the meeting.

$1.115M Overrun — The Sheridan Drive Sidewalk Bond Climbs to $2,015,466

The board's first substantive vote of the morning was a five-page bond resolution amending financing for sidewalks on both sides of Sheridan Drive between Thompson/Main and Transit Road — five miles of sidewalk total. The original 2022 NYSDOT-grant estimate was $4,310,035; with detailed design and after bids opened April 30, the estimate now stands at $5,463,494. The town's share (the bonded portion) goes from $900,000 originally authorized in May 2025 up to $2,015,466 — a $1,115,466 increase passed by roll call.[1]

Supervisor Casilio asked the explanation from highway superintendent Tim, who noted the original estimating predated detailed design and that bids were opened on April 30. "The result of those bids are reflected in this increased bond authorization."[1] Contract award is expected at a future meeting.

"Either Incomplete or Inaccurate" — Casilio Reads a Bee-Bound Article Into the Record

Mid-meeting, Casilio paused the resolutions calendar to read into the record an article he said will appear in this week's Bee. The subject: a possible municipal garbage collection district. The trigger: comments made at the April town board meeting by residents advocating for one. The framing: "Information shared in support of these claims was either incomplete or inaccurate, resulting in a mischaracterization of the facts."[1]

The town has retained Drescher & Malecki LLP[*] — the Buffalo-based municipal accounting firm — for an "objective comparison" using communities comparable in size. Hamburg and Orchard Park were named as the comparables under study. Casilio noted Hamburg has recently established a district but is not yet fully operational, and Orchard Park just completed what he called "a lengthy and complex process to secure a new vendor."[1]

His numbers, attributed to preliminary findings: a municipal district in Clarence would cost approximately $320 per year — "slightly less than what private haulers are charging now" — but with "far less services" than residents currently receive. He named two existing private haulers and their rates: Waste Management at $462 per year with recycling every other week (bulk pickup extra); Modern Disposal at $540 per year with weekly recycling and weekly bulk pickup.[1]

"The people that came in and spoke before us said that these vendors are charging $1,100 a year… It's important for our residents to always keep checking their pricing, because the vendors have restructured their pricing to be more competitive."[1]

Then the fiscal warning: implementing a district could increase town expenditures by as much as $4 million in the first year alone — enough to bust the New York State 2% tax cap. "Passing this cap could result in many residents possibly losing their STAR property tax credits. For most people STAR could be worth $900. You're going to save $100 on your garbage refuge, but lose your STAR credit of $900 — or even more depending upon your age and your service to the community."[1]

Twenty years ago, Casilio noted, the town considered a district and roughly 3,800 residents voted on it; the proposal was rejected by 333 votes. He closed with a procedural promise: when Drescher & Malecki finishes its report, "certainly we'll be bringing it forward to the public."[1]

"Discussions of this nature must be grounded in accurate and viable information. Before bringing concerns to the media or attempting to shape public perception, it is important to ensure that the facts are fully understood and correctly presented."Supervisor Patrick Casilio, reading his Bee article[1]
"One of the reasons I read this whole thing out is Sean's doing billable hours. Are you charging him for all this time?"Casilio, mid-article, to town counsel Sean Hopkins[1]
